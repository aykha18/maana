# Ma'na Interpretation And Provenance Model v1

## Purpose

This document defines how Ma'na should govern meaning.

It does not define user interface, database structure, or output formatting.

Its role is to define the rules by which:

- claims are formed
- evidence is attached
- provenance is preserved
- truth is scoped
- disagreement is represented
- canonicality is governed

This document exists because Ma'na's central problem is not merely storing literature.

It is preserving meaning without confusing:

- fact with interpretation
- elegance with evidence
- consensus with truth
- AI fluency with authority

## Scope

This model applies to all claims in Ma'na, including:

- factual claims
- textual claims
- historical claims
- interpretive claims
- symbolic claims
- comparative claims
- ontological mappings
- graph relationships
- AI-generated hypotheses
- editorial decisions

## Foundational Principle

Every meaningful claim in Ma'na must be governable.

To be governable, a claim must be:

- addressable
- typed
- scoped
- evidenced
- provenance-bearing
- reviewable
- contestable

Any statement that lacks these properties may still exist as a note, draft, or prompt, but it must not operate as canonical knowledge.

## Claim Model

## 1. What A Claim Is

A claim is an assertion about an addressable object within Ma'na.

That object may be:

- a source
- a witness
- a segment
- a unit
- a work
- an ontology entity
- another claim
- a relationship

Claims are the principal carriers of knowledge in Ma'na.

## 2. Required Properties Of Every Claim

Every claim must have, conceptually, the following:

- target
- claim type
- scope
- status
- provenance
- evidence posture

### Target

What the claim is about.

### Claim Type

What kind of assertion it is.

### Scope

Under what conditions it should be interpreted as valid.

### Status

Its editorial or epistemic standing.

### Provenance

Who or what made it, by what method, from what source context.

### Evidence Posture

Whether the claim is directly evidenced, indirectly supported, tradition-backed, speculative, or unsupported.

## Claim Types

The following claim classes should be treated as distinct.

They may interact, but they must not collapse into one undifferentiated commentary layer.

## 1. Textual Claim

A claim about what a source or witness contains.

### Examples

- this line appears in this edition
- this word occurs in this position
- this witness omits the second hemistich

### Nature

This is one of the strongest claim types because it is anchored closest to source material.

## 2. Editorial Claim

A claim introduced by editorial judgment.

### Examples

- this segment should be treated as a meaningful unit
- this passage boundary is preferable
- this witness is the base text for commentary

### Nature

Editorial claims are necessary, but they are governed judgments, not raw facts.

## 3. Historical Claim

A claim about date, authorship, transmission, context, or historical background.

### Examples

- this commentary emerged in the late twentieth century
- this edition derives from a known manuscript tradition

### Nature

Historical claims require historical evidence and remain revisable.

## 4. Descriptive Claim

A claim that describes observable features of a text without yet moving into interpretation.

### Examples

- this passage shifts from hope to irony
- the imagery of wine recurs repeatedly

### Nature

Descriptive claims may be interpretively adjacent but should remain separable from stronger symbolic or philosophical assertions.

## 5. Interpretive Claim

A claim about the meaning of a work, unit, image, symbol, relation, or question.

### Examples

- this couplet expresses existential exhaustion
- this scene dramatizes indecision
- this image suggests spiritual distance

### Nature

Interpretive claims are central to Ma'na, but they must never silently become factual claims.

## 6. Comparative Claim

A claim that relates two or more works, units, traditions, symbols, or concepts.

### Examples

- this image resembles Hafiz more than Iqbal
- this question parallels Kierkegaard's treatment of despair

### Nature

Comparative claims are powerful but vulnerable to overreach.

They require especially careful provenance and evidence.

## 7. Ontological Claim

A claim that maps a work or unit to a reusable ontology entity.

### Examples

- this passage evokes longing
- this image instantiates the wine symbol
- this line should map to the concept of exile

### Nature

These claims should almost never become canonical automatically.

They are high-impact because they affect reuse, retrieval, and graph structure.

## 8. Relational Claim

A claim that asserts a meaningful relationship between two entities.

### Examples

- this claim supports another claim
- this interpretation challenges a prior reading
- this symbol contrasts with another symbol

### Nature

Relational claims should be governed with the same seriousness as ordinary claims.

## 9. Inferential Claim

A claim proposed through synthesis, analytical inference, or AI reasoning.

### Examples

- this pattern may suggest an unresolved theological tension
- these works may share a deeper structure of exile and selfhood

### Nature

Inferential claims are valuable, but must remain explicitly inferential unless later reviewed and stabilized.

## Evidence Model

## 1. Principle

Evidence is not decoration.

Evidence is what constrains interpretation.

If a claim cannot state what supports it, the claim must not masquerade as stable knowledge.

## 2. Evidence Types

At minimum, Ma'na should recognize the following evidence classes.

### Textual Evidence

Quoted spans, word usage, structural pattern, or witness comparison.

### Lexical Evidence

Dictionary history, root analysis, semantic range, usage tradition.

### Historical Evidence

Publication record, manuscript witness, date, biography, documented context.

### Scholarly Evidence

Commentary tradition, published criticism, academic argument, attributed interpretation.

### Comparative Evidence

Parallel passages, literary analogues, philosophical echoes, shared motifs.

### Traditional Evidence

Claims grounded in a recognized interpretive tradition such as tafsir, Sufi commentary, philology, or literary criticism.

### Methodological Evidence

Declared method, such as close reading, thematic synthesis, computational clustering, ontology mapping, or AI inference.

## 3. Evidence Postures

Every claim should be able to indicate one of the following evidence postures:

- directly evidenced
- strongly supported
- plausibly supported
- tradition-backed
- inferential
- speculative
- unsupported

These are not UI labels.

They are governance distinctions.

## 4. Negative Principle

A persuasive tone is never evidence.

Rhetorical coherence is never sufficient support.

Popularity is never evidence by itself.

## Provenance Model

## 1. Provenance Principle

No meaningful claim should enter Ma'na without provenance.

Provenance is required not only for trust, but for reversibility, contestability, and long-term scholarly integrity.

## 2. Provenance Questions

Every claim should be answerable against the following questions:

- who or what created this claim?
- what type of contributor created it?
- from which source or witness context?
- by what method?
- at what time?
- under what assumptions or framework?
- using which tools or models, if any?
- reviewed by whom?
- revised from what prior state?

## 3. Provenance For AI

AI-generated claims require stricter provenance than ordinary editorial notes.

They should preserve:

- model identity
- model version or family
- prompting context class
- transformation type
- evidence inputs
- post-processing steps
- reviewer actions

If these cannot be recovered, the result should not be allowed to behave as canonical knowledge.

## Truth And Scope Model

## 1. Truth Requires Scope

Ma'na should almost never present a claim as universally true without scope.

Claims are true only within identifiable boundaries.

### Possible Scopes

- for this witness
- for this edition
- for this tradition
- for this scholarly consensus
- for this interpretive framework
- for this editorial model

## 2. Truth Status Categories

The system should be able to distinguish conceptually among:

- verified
- supported
- contested
- disputed
- unresolved
- speculative
- inferred
- deprecated

These are not the same as workflow states.

They are epistemic statuses.

## 3. Editorial States Versus Truth Status

The following must not be confused:

- whether a claim is approved
- whether a claim is true

A claim may be:

- approved for inclusion
- yet still contested in truth-status

This distinction is essential for scholarly integrity.

## Interpretation Model

## 1. Interpretation Is A Special Claim Class

Interpretation is a claim about meaning, not a blob of prose.

This means an interpretation can:

- cite evidence
- belong to a tradition
- be challenged
- be compared
- be revised
- be canonical within scope
- remain non-canonical globally

## 2. Interpretation Modes

Ma'na should distinguish at least the following interpretation modes:

- literal
- descriptive
- paraphrastic
- symbolic
- emotional
- psychological
- existential
- mystical
- philosophical
- comparative
- pedagogical
- synthetic

These modes may overlap, but should never be treated as interchangeable.

## 3. Interpretation Boundaries

An interpretation must not silently cross from one mode into another without clarity.

For example:

- symbolic reading is not the same as textual description
- mystical interpretation is not the same as consensus reading
- AI synthesis is not the same as inherited tradition

## 4. Tradition-Aware Interpretation

Some interpretations belong explicitly to traditions.

The model must allow a claim to say:

- this is a Sufi reading
- this is a philological reading
- this is a modern literary-critical reading
- this is an AI synthesis across prior sources

Without this, plurality collapses into false neutrality.

## Disagreement Model

## 1. Disagreement Is A First-Class State

Ma'na must preserve disagreement explicitly rather than burying it in prose.

## 2. Forms Of Disagreement

Disagreement may arise because:

- evidence is incomplete
- editions differ
- methods differ
- traditions differ
- ontology mapping is contested
- AI and human readings diverge

## 3. Permitted Coexistence

The model must allow:

- multiple supported interpretations
- mutually incompatible traditions
- unresolved editorial judgments
- disputed ontology assignments
- rival explanatory frameworks

## 4. Forbidden Collapse

The system must not:

- merge disagreement for smooth UX
- prefer fluency over fidelity
- present minority claims as consensus
- suppress ambiguity because it is difficult to model

## Canonicality Boundaries

## 1. What Canonicality Means Here

Canonicality is permission for stable reuse.

It is not proof of eternal truth.

## 2. What May Become Canonical

Depending on governance, the following may become canonical:

- ontology entities
- stable textual references
- approved editorial unit boundaries
- well-supported commentary claims within scope
- curated cross-reference links

## 3. What Must Not Become Canonical Automatically

The following must never become canonical merely because they exist:

- AI opinions
- speculative symbolic readings
- unsupported ontology mappings
- unattributed explanations
- inferred cross-civilizational equivalences
- synthesized answers with no recoverable evidence base

## 4. Canonical Within Scope

Some claims may become canonical only within a limited scope.

Examples:

- canonical for a specific edition
- canonical within a specific tradition
- canonical for house editorial usage

This is not weakness.

It is truthful governance.

## Review And Governance Model

## 1. Every High-Impact Claim Requires Review

The higher the downstream impact of a claim, the stricter the review requirement.

High-impact claims include:

- ontology mappings
- graph relations
- cross-work equivalences
- canonical commentary claims
- claims used for retrieval or recommendation

## 2. Low-Impact Versus High-Impact Claims

Not all claims require identical review depth.

### Lower-Impact

- draft paraphrase
- private editorial note
- temporary AI summary

### Higher-Impact

- canonical ontology link
- approved interpretation used in public explanation
- graph-forming relation
- tradition-labeled commentary

## 3. Reversibility

Every reviewed claim should remain reversible.

Ma'na must be able to answer:

- why was this approved?
- from what evidence?
- under which model?
- by whose authority?
- what replaced it, if deprecated?

## AI Governance

## 1. Permitted AI Roles

AI may:

- extract candidate claims
- summarize approved knowledge
- cluster related passages
- propose ontology mappings
- identify tensions or open questions
- generate hypotheses for review
- compose explanations from approved claim sets

## 2. Restricted AI Roles

AI must not autonomously:

- finalize canonical ontology
- finalize truth status
- erase contradictory evidence
- fabricate provenance
- conceal uncertainty
- flatten traditions into one voice

## 3. AI Output Classes

AI output should always be classifiable as one of:

- extraction
- summary
- synthesis
- interpretation
- inference
- hypothesis

These classes must remain distinguishable because they carry different governance burdens.

## Consequences For Commentary

This model implies that commentary is not a primitive truth object.

Instead, commentary is a composed artifact built from:

- typed claims
- scoped truth
- explicit evidence
- provenance records
- visible disagreement
- ontology links

Therefore the future canonical commentary schema must be derived from this model, not the other way around.

## Minimum Invariants For Future Schemas

Any future schema or workflow violates this model if it:

- stores interpretation without provenance
- stores claims without scope
- stores ontology mappings without reviewability
- presents AI synthesis without evidence lineage
- hides disagreement inside a single authoritative paragraph
- treats approval as equivalent to universal truth
